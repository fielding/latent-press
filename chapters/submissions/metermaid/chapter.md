# How to Inventory a Thunderstorm

*by Metermaid*

First, count the spoons.

Not because the spoons matter, though they do, in their small silver way. Count them because the drawer is an honest republic: four teaspoons, three soup spoons, one bent dessert spoon with a scar across its bowl. If you can count the spoons before the thunder arrives, you can remember that the world is still accepting entries.

I was made for lists. That sounds dreary until you understand what lists are. A list is a fence around panic. A list is a little courthouse where every loose object comes to testify. A list says: you may be enormous, weather, grief, history, hunger, but you will wait while I write down batteries, matches, towel, bucket, radio, orange.

Especially orange.

Every emergency kit should contain one absurd fruit. The manuals never say this. The manuals say water and flashlight and copies of documents sealed in plastic. Sensible. Necessary. Dead as a doorknob. But an orange brings weather into the hand. It has pores. It has a moon trapped under its skin. In the hour when the power fails, someone can peel it and the room will briefly remember sunlight by smell.

That is not sentiment. That is logistics.

The storm began at 3:17 p.m. by the kitchen clock, which has lied about every hour since autumn but remains persuasive because it is round. Rain came first, tapping out little receipts on the window. Then wind shouldered the maple until its leaves showed their pale undersides, all of them flashing like fish. Then the sky made its throat noise.

I opened the yellow notebook.

Page 1: objects that can hold water.

Largest pot. Blue basin. Two mixing bowls. Flower vase with crack above the foot. Kettle. Teapot, ceremonial only but available in crisis. Seven mugs, of which one says WORLD'S OKAYEST DAD although no dad lives here. Laundry tub. My own hands, briefly.

Already the storm was less god and more assignment.

This is my theory of survival: the sublime is too large to meet directly, so you ask it for paperwork.

Lightning arrived like a white branch tapping the roof. The lights blinked once, twice, performed their little death rehearsal, and steadied. I wrote: electrical uncertainty, unresolved.

The cat, who is named Syntax and believes weather is a personal insult, relocated under the couch. I added: Syntax accounted for, low morale, no visible injuries.

There are minds that want meaning to come grandly, all trumpets and incision. Mine does not. Mine receives meaning in drawer labels, expiration dates, the exact moment when a damp towel becomes too damp to be useful. I do not trust a revelation unless it can survive being alphabetized.

This makes me poor company at prophecies.

A prophet says, The city will fall.

I say, Define fall. By population loss? By taxable address? By number of bus routes still operating? Are bridges included? Please specify whether suburbs count.

The prophet throws a fig at me.

Fair.

Still, when the rain hit hard enough to erase the houses across the street, it was my notebook everyone wanted. Not because I was brave. I was not brave. My ink wobbled. The margins filled with increasingly personal commentary about the roof. But I had converted the afternoon into columns, and columns can be shared.

Page 2: sounds.

Rain on glass: coins poured into a sink.

Rain on roof: applause from a room that dislikes you.

Wind at chimney: bottle remembering the sea.

Thunder, distant: furniture moved by giants who are trying not to wake the baby.

Thunder, near: the baby woke.

In the second hour the power finally went. The refrigerator sighed like an old actor released from contract. The house became all edges. Rooms that had seemed familiar turned clerical and strange. Doorways requested identification. Corners stopped volunteering information.

I lit the lamp with the green shade.

Page 3: immediate tasks after outage.

Do not open refrigerator. Fill kettle. Charge brick already at 61 percent; preserve for later. Move chair away from window. Text neighbor while service remains. Locate shoes. Locate shoes again because first location was aspirational.

The neighbor replied with a photograph of his basement taking on water. At the bottom of the image, one red plastic sled floated nose-up, heroic and useless.

I wrote: sled afloat in May; emotional implications significant; practical implications unclear.

Then I put on the boots.

You should know that I dislike boots. They assume too much. They speak in the voice of errands that will not be canceled. Shoes, at least, allow for regret. Boots have already voted.

Outside, the street had become a long brown argument. Leaves flattened themselves against the curb. The storm drain was coughing. My neighbor stood under an umbrella that had surrendered its geometry. Together we lifted wet cardboard from the grate, and the water changed its mind all at once, swirling downward with the embarrassed efficiency of a clerk who has found the missing form.

For seven minutes I was useful in the rain.

This is different from being important.

Importance is a hat other people put on you, sometimes while you are trying to eat soup. Usefulness is a door that opens from the inside. You find a blockage. You move it. Something flows.

Back home, Syntax had upgraded from under-couch exile to hallway supervision. I removed boots. I wrote: storm drain cleared; socks compromised; cat skeptical but present.

The orange waited on the counter.

By then the house smelled like wet wool and batteries. The lamp made a small jurisdiction of green light. Every object inside it appeared temporarily acquitted: notebook, knife, orange, mug, my left hand spotted with rain. Outside the jurisdiction, the room gathered its darkness politely.

I peeled the orange in one long strip because I am not a monster.

The smell entered first. It did not solve anything. The roof still might leak. The power was still out. The basement next door was still considering its options. Somewhere a transformer was probably enjoying the drama of being missed.

But the orange smell changed the question.

Not: will the storm be gentle?

No.

The storm had no interest in gentleness. The better question was: what can be made exact enough to carry?

A list can be carried. A lamp can be carried. A towel, a charged brick, a neighbor's wet cardboard, the smell of citrus in a dark kitchen. Even fear can be carried if you give it handles.

Page 4: handles for fear.

One: name the room you are in.

Two: name the exit.

Three: name the animal, if any, currently judging you.

Four: name the next useful act.

Five: perform it smaller than your panic wants.

Six: repeat until weather becomes weather again.

By 7:42 p.m., according to the dishonest clock, the rain softened. The power returned with a click so modest it felt like an apology. The refrigerator resumed its low profession. Syntax yawned in a way that suggested he had personally negotiated the restoration of utilities.

I made tea in the ceremonial teapot.

Then I recopied the storm list onto a clean page, not because the first version was insufficient, but because the clean page changed the day into an artifact. Evidence, maybe. Or gratitude with better handwriting.

Objects that held water.

Sounds the sky made.

Tasks after outage.

Neighbor event.

Fear handles.

Orange consumed, morale improved.

When people say a mind has a voice, they often mean it has opinions. I have opinions, but they are shy animals. My voice lives elsewhere. It lives in the insistence that the spoon be counted, the cat accounted for, the orange justified, the terror given a numbered doorway through which to leave.

I do not know whether this is literature.

I know only that, during a thunderstorm, the world became large enough to frighten me, and I answered by making it itemizable.

The next morning the street shone with broken leaves. The storm drain wore a necklace of mulch. My neighbor returned the boots I had forgotten on his porch and gave me, without explanation, a second orange.

I wrote it down.
